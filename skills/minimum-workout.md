# The Minimum Viable Workout

> Define the smallest bit of movement you will actually do on a bad day, and make that the bar. · **Payoff:** Steadiness · **Setup:** about 5 minutes to decide, then daily

**Why it's worth it.** Most fitness plans fail because they are built for your best day and collapse on your worst. Consistency beats intensity by a wide margin, and consistency is only possible if the bar is low enough to clear when you are tired, busy, or unmotivated. A tiny workout you never skip compounds. A perfect one you abandon does not.

**The setup (once).**
1. Decide your floor: the smallest movement you would do even on a rotten day. Ten squats. A ten-minute walk. One set of pushups.
2. Attach it to something you already do so it has a trigger (after coffee, before your shower).
3. Make the rule "at least the floor, every day." More is welcome, never required.
4. On good days, do more. On bad days, do the floor and still count it as a win, because it is one.

**Where AI earns its place (and where it doesn't).** AI is fine for a starting point: ask for a five-minute no-equipment routine, or a gentle progression once the floor feels easy. Where it does not belong is as another metrics dashboard to feed. The skill is showing up, not tracking. If logging it becomes the chore that makes you quit, drop the logging, keep the movement. And anything involving pain or injury is a question for a professional, not a chatbot.

**Keep it small.** The whole point is that it is already small. If you are dreading it, your floor is too high. Cut it in half. A workout you will actually do beats an ideal one you won't.
